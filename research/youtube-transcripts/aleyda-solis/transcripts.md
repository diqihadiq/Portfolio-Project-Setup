To wrap up, I want to share my own SEO tip for 2026 and it is to every time that you do anything - create content, optimize content, enable a new section, take actions to improve the indexibility, crawlability, rendering of your content - anything is to ask if this is aligned with your brand. Digital PR, community management - we can see how a lot of the answers from LLM citations are coming from these external sources. ## Video 2

**Title:** SEO FOMO Newsletter Breakdown: The Great Decoupling, Zero-Click Search & AI Content Optimization (Crawling Mondays with Jono Alderson)
**URL:** https://youtu.be/N1Eup1sH1fQ
**Date:** 2025
**Duration:** ~30 min

**Transcript:**

[On the Great Decoupling]

The first story is the coverage from Barry Schwarz about the great decoupling explained by a Googler Martin Split who was at Google Search Central last week where he explained the trend going on due to the AI overviews that is being even further accelerated in many scenarios with the AI mode.

The decoupling - the traffic, the clicks that we see in Google Search Console from the impressions of the site - many more impressions, fewer clicks - due to the type of conversational engagement and interaction that users have. The term was coined by Darwin Santos and it's very descriptive of what we can see.

I don't know if you have seen the same with your clients - I work a lot with e-commerce and marketplaces and SaaS, so very transactional oriented websites, and I haven't yet seen a trend as extreme as the one Martin is showing in his deck. I am seeing those much more on informational sections of the websites that I work on. Thankfully because they are very commercially oriented and most of the activity doesn't happen in there, the impact is not quite as high. But I understand that it's very very different with publishers and publications and those players that rely a lot on informational content, especially if their business model is completely relying on that.

[Jono Alderson responds:]

I thought what was really interesting is this idea that even if you're getting fewer clicks they're going to be higher value, they're going to be more likely to convert. That bit makes me super nervous. I think that feels like quite a reductive e-commerce-centric lens and it assumes that every site is trying to sell something or should have an explicit user outcome. It's almost like they're saying, we've stripped out all of those pesky, curious audience members who weren't quite ready to hit convert or buy now, and we're only sending you people who are right at the bottom of the funnel. Which may be great if you're treating this as a performance space, but we all know that brand and general marketing and audience cultivation is so much bigger than just get them to click my thing and convert them.

[Aleyda on the incentive problem:]

Even if you are not a publication, if you are a merchant, an online store - what are the incentives now to create informational content that is very well optimized, that targets and connects with the way that the user searches to clarify the doubts to help them through the whole customer journey? It's zero. Because if everything will go just to the bottom of the funnel directly and most of the traffic will eventually be referred to the PDP, you don't need massive informational content. And we know that it's a chicken and egg type of situation because it's not only traditional search but also AI search - LLMs rely on informational content and citations play an even bigger role there in order to gauge authority and relevance. So the incentives are not there, there's a shift certainly happening.

[On Google Search Console and AI mode data:]

Google has now added AI mode traffic to the Google Search Console reports - however the big thing is that it's all blended with the organic traditional organic search metrics. What I have seen is that a few have started to create reports or filter reports with queries only showcasing queries more than five words or so. But we know that Google aizes a lot of the more specific longer tail type of terms.

I would much prefer that Google provided filters like they do for money results. We also need OpenAI and other platforms to give clarity and visibility on the user search activity or engagement and traffic they refer.

[On zero-click search:]

The guide from Ahrefs showcases - welcome to the zero-click search era. It goes through all of the culprits for this: AI overviews, AI mode. And what should we do to navigate this in a way that is not negative for the final outcome?

The recommendations: developing products or user focused content, creating deliberately human content, going deep with customer research, diversifying to new channels, going all in on brand and PR.

[Jono on brand and PR as the key response:]

Annoyingly as a technical SEO, the bit that resonated with me most was this idea that we should be doubling down on brand and PR and that some of the most influential factors for fighting to survive in this change are citations, links, references. Either you're what these AI systems and LLMs are understanding and believing about your product proposition, or instead of trying to say how do we rank better for things that people are searching for, you change the narrative and you say - what if we did a whole bunch of marketing that changes how people search and changes what they search for? Or we double the number of people who search for our brand instead of trying to make this keyword rank fifth instead of sixth. It's such a more grown up way of thinking about search as an ecosystem and less as a performance channel.

[Aleyda on Kevin Indig's piece on the decoupling:]

Kevin Indig also wrote a piece going through many screenshots of websites with the great decoupling trend happening for them. He recommends taking certain steps in order to still get visibility, still get influence in those queries that actually matter - those that have the right intent. Going through paid media, social and community, Reddit marketing, content, and GTM.

There's a phrase towards the bottom which might be the best thing I've ever read in an SEO context - needing higher insight density per paragraph. If we're going to be assessed by these systems, if we're going to be fighting for fewer clicks, all of these things boil down to: we have to make our stuff so much better with so much less surface area. More insight in every paragraph.

[Aleyda on her AI search content optimization checklist:]

What I did was to summarize the top actions connected with every area that I saw was relevant to optimize content for AI search:

- Chunk level retrieval: optimizing your passages and snippets of content to make them semantically tied to really answer and address specific topics, specific facets of queries, rather than trying to rank at page level.
- Optimized for answer synthesis and citation worthiness.
- Topical breadth and depth.

The key point is it's not purely about the concepts or the areas of optimization, but HOW we optimize them. Because the retrieval methodology changes, the behavior of the users changes, the interface is different, we need to measure it differently, and the outcome we expect is different too.

It's important to showcase examples to allow people to understand how it should fit with content created for users and for your brand - for the sake of a real business behind - not trying to rank for everything and anything just because it's a facet of a query and because of the fan out technique it might get triggered at some point.

[Jono on paragraph-level thinking:]

People already struggle with page level thinking. They assume that everybody neatly enters a website on the homepage, reads everything about the business, and then linearly navigates through a site and makes a purchase decision. When the reality is chaotic. People arrive on a random deep page and don't know anything about the company or the product, and the content on that page almost always assumes context they don't have.

Now there's this extra level of: you've got to take it down to paragraph level. Should this be one page or two for a topic? That becomes: should this be one paragraph? It's far harder. But this is what we should always have been aspiring to anyway.

[On synthetic authority - Jono's take:]

When you deconstruct the E-E-A-T stuff about experience, expertise, authority and trust - all of those things you can get close to and maybe surpass humans with a machine. I want to buy a new air fryer - today I definitely want to read a bunch of reviews by an expert who has touched a hundred of them. But it's not hard to imagine a scenario where a piece of software tests and evaluates the airflow of a thousand air fryers a million times and uses AI to evaluate enough spot patterns. It's not hard to imagine a scenario where that does a far better job than any single expert ever could. At what point do we start to accept that and maybe normalize it? Human content becomes average and the AI-synthesized stuff is actually the really great stuff.

[Aleyda on AI-generated content from proprietary data:]

For websites that already have a lot of UGC, adding an AI layer that is able to eliminate bias and identify the patterns for a summary - I have already seen this in a more primitive way taking place across online stores with thousands of reviews on PDPs and AI summaries at the top going through the positives and negatives mentioned in the reviews below. Tools like AirOps or Warlift that integrate with your own content and database - based on what they learn in a personalized way and based on your own workflows and criteria, they end up generating content that reflects the data and insights you have. We're still in early stages, but the tools are already capable to do that. What is still important is to have a validation layer for the sake of accuracy.

## Key Themes from These Transcripts

- Brand alignment is the #1 SEO priority for 2026 - every content and optimization decision should be asked "is this on brand?"
- The Great Decoupling is real: impressions stay high but clicks are collapsing, especially for informational content
- Zero-click era requires a strategic response: brand and PR, not more content production
- AI mode traffic in Google Search Console is blended with organic - currently no way to separate it cleanly
- Incentive problem: if AI absorbs top-of-funnel queries, publishers and informational sites lose the economic model for creating content
- Content optimization must shift from page-level to paragraph/chunk-level for AI retrieval
- Higher insight density per paragraph is the key metric for AI-era content quality
- Synthetic authority is coming: AI systems may eventually produce more trustworthy content than human experts on data-heavy topics
- Citations, links, and external references (Reddit, forums, community) are increasingly what LLMs use to gauge authority
- Agentic web is the next frontier: commerce protocol, A2A protocol, payment protocol all converging in 2026
- Proprietary data is becoming a strategic asset - companies that own unique data can potentially license it to LLMs
